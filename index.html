Origins

A Prince Becomes a King

In 1930, a regent named Tafari Makonnen was crowned Emperor of Ethiopia, taking the name Haile Selassie I. To followers across the Caribbean, his coronation read as a sign — proof that Africa still held a living king, at a moment when scripture's promise of a returning sovereign felt close enough to touch.

1892

Born Lij Tafari Makonnen in Ejersa Goro, Ethiopia, into a noble family with ties to the Solomonic dynasty.

1916

Named Ras — a title close to duke or prince — and made Regent of Ethiopia.

1930

Crowned Emperor, taking the throne name Haile Selassie I along with titles including King of Kings and Conquering Lion of the Tribe of Judah.

1935–36

Fascist Italy invades Ethiopia; Selassie's appeal to the League of Nations makes him a global symbol of resistance to colonial rule.

1966

Visits Jamaica for the first time. Tens of thousands gather at the airport — a day still marked by Rastafari as Grounation Day.

1974–75

Deposed in a military revolution and dies in custody. Many Rastafari hold that his spiritual significance is untouched by his physical death.

Faith & Practice

Zion and Babylon

Rastafari is less an organized church than a shared way of reading scripture, history, and the body. A few ideas run through nearly every branch of it:

Ethiopianism

A belief, older than the movement itself, that Ethiopia's mention in the Bible marked it as a sign of Black redemption — and that Africa, not Europe, was the true Zion.

Babylon

The shorthand for every oppressive system: colonial rule, the established church, and the societies built on the long history of the slave trade.

Ital Living

A diet and lifestyle kept close to nature — plant-based, unprocessed, free of salt or additives — meant to keep body and spirit clean.

Dreadlocks

Hair worn long and natural as a kind of covenant, echoing Old Testament Nazarite vows and the look of Ethiopian warriors before it became a global symbol.

Nyabinghi

Drumming, chanting, and reasoning sessions that form the movement's central act of worship, named after a historic East African resistance tradition.

The Holy Herb

Cannabis, used in worship and reasoning as an aid to insight, with believers pointing to biblical passages describing plants given for healing.

Colors & Signs

Red, Gold, Green — and Black

The Rastafari palette is borrowed from Ethiopia's flag and stretched with new meaning.

Black

Africa, and the people who descend from it.

Red

The blood shed in centuries of struggle against oppression.

Gold

The wealth and sunlight of the African homeland.

Green

Ethiopia's land, vegetation, and hope for what comes next.

Alongside the colors, the Lion of Judah — Ethiopia's old imperial emblem — stands in for Haile Selassie himself, and for a strength rooted in faith rather than force.

Reaching the World

Reggae Carried the Word

In 1970s Jamaica, reggae became Rastafari's loudest pulpit. Studio sessions and street sound systems carried reasonings about Zion, repatriation, and resistance far past the hills where the faith began.

Musicians steeped in the movement — leaning on lion imagery, Ethiopian colors, and biblical language — turned a regional faith into a sound heard on every continent, long after most listeners had any direct contact with Jamaica itself. Bob Marley remains the name most people meet first; behind him is a much wider tradition of Nyabinghi drummers, deejays, and reasoners who kept the faith's vocabulary alive in music.
